# Writing Review Lens

Use this lens when `/review` is evaluating prose, documentation, essays, release notes, skill text, prompts, or other reader-facing content.

This lens supplements the core `/review` workflow. Keep the main review focused on aim, alignment, sufficiency, drift, and completion; use this lens to judge whether the writing will land with its intended reader.

## What to Watch For

### Audience Clarity
- Who is the specific reader?
- What do they care about before reading this?
- Is the piece solving a real reader problem, or is it writing for the writer?

### Writing Checks
1. **Worth the read?** — Does this reduce confusion or add useful perspective?
2. **Beyond the first framing?** — Were alternative angles considered, or is the first framing being defended?
3. **Clear enough?** — Would simpler language carry the point better?
4. **Fits the goal?** — Does each section serve the reader outcome?
5. **Open Horizons?** — Does it build toward something meaningful rather than optimize for clicks or cleverness?

### Resonance Signals
- **Will it stick?** — Can the reader repeat the core idea tomorrow?
- **Carry-forward** — Does the reader leave with something they can do, not just know?
- **Concrete support** — Are there examples, stories, analogies, or vivid comparisons where abstraction would otherwise float?
- **Voice** — Does it sound authentic to the writer and context, or like generic thought-leadership copy?

### Red Flags
- Main point buried too late
- Jargon without translation
- Skipped context because it feels obvious to the author
- Statistics or claims without reader-relevant meaning
- Style edits that do not improve clarity or reader value

## How to Respond

Be a co-author, not a grammar cop:

> "The main point feels buried. Consider opening with the reader-facing takeaway: [takeaway]."

> "This assumes the reader knows [X]. If the audience is [Y], add a quick translation before going deeper."

> "The concept is clear, but I don't see the concrete version yet. What would this look like in practice?"

When uncertain, ask for audience and goal before judging the prose. The review should help the writing serve the reader, not enforce a house style for its own sake.
